Why Convert Images to PDF?

Images are great for viewing individually, but they have limitations when it comes to sharing, printing, and archiving. Sending 15 separate JPG files as email attachments is awkward. Printing a collection of photos with consistent page sizes and margins requires a formatted document. Archiving a set of receipts, whiteboard photos, or scanned pages as loose image files makes them harder to find and organize.

PDF solves these problems. By converting images to PDF, you get a single, organized document that renders identically on every device, prints with consistent formatting, and can be annotated, signed, and shared as a single file.

Common reasons to convert images to PDF:

  • Document scanning: Combine photos of paper documents into a single, organized PDF for filing or sharing.
  • Photo portfolios: Create a clean, professional portfolio document from a collection of images.
  • Receipts and invoices: Consolidate photos of receipts into a single PDF for expense reports or tax records.
  • Presentations: Turn a set of slide images into a paginated PDF handout.
  • Proof sheets: Arrange multiple images on pages for review or approval workflows.

Supported Image Formats Explained

Not all image formats are created equal. Understanding the differences helps you choose the right input for the best output.

Format Compression Transparency Best For
JPG / JPEG Lossy No Photos, scanned documents, camera images
PNG Lossless Yes Screenshots, graphics, logos, text-heavy images
WebP Both Yes Web-optimized images, modern screenshots
BMP None No Legacy images, raw captures
GIF Lossless (256 colors) Yes (1-bit) Simple graphics (first frame only for animated GIFs)
TIFF Various Yes High-quality scans, print production

For most conversions, JPG and PNG are the formats you will work with. JPGs are smaller but use lossy compression that can introduce visible artifacts around edges and text. PNGs are larger but preserve every pixel exactly.

Tip

If you are photographing documents with your phone camera, the JPG format is fine. For screenshots or computer-generated graphics, PNG produces sharper results in the final PDF.

Page Size, Orientation, and Margins

Standard Page Sizes

  • A4 (210 x 297 mm): Standard international paper size. Best for documents intended for printing in Europe, Asia, and most of the world.
  • Letter (8.5 x 11 in): Standard US paper size. Best for documents intended for printing in the US and Canada.
  • Fit to Image: The page size matches the image dimensions exactly. Best for digital viewing where print margins are irrelevant.

Margins

Adding margins creates white space around the image on each page. This is important for printing, where content at the very edge of a page may be cut off. A margin of 10-15 mm on each side is typical for printable documents. For screen-only viewing, zero margins give you the most image area.

Image Scaling

When an image does not match the page aspect ratio, the tool needs to decide how to handle the difference. The options typically include:

  • Fit: Scale the image to fit within the page while maintaining its aspect ratio. This may leave white bars on the sides or top/bottom.
  • Fill: Scale the image to fill the entire page, which may crop the edges.
  • Stretch: Stretch the image to exactly match the page dimensions. This distorts the image and is rarely desirable.
  • Original: Place the image at its original pixel size without scaling. Large images may extend beyond the page boundaries.

Image Quality and File Size Tips

Optimizing Before Conversion

If your images are very large (for example, 4000 x 3000 pixels from a modern camera), they will produce a large PDF. For screen viewing, you do not need images larger than 1920 x 1080 pixels per page. Resize your images before converting to save space.

Compressing After Conversion

If the resulting PDF is too large for email or upload limits, use the PDF Compress tool to reduce the file size. Moderate compression can cut the file size by 50-70% with minimal visible quality impact.

Watch the File Size

A PDF with 50 high-resolution photos can easily exceed 100 MB. If you plan to share the file via email (typically 10-25 MB attachment limit), compress the PDF or resize the images first.

Choosing the Right Input Format

JPG images compress well and produce smaller PDFs, but may show compression artifacts. PNG images produce larger PDFs but with pixel-perfect quality. For photos and camera images, JPG is fine. For screenshots, diagrams, and text-heavy images, use PNG for the sharpest results.
